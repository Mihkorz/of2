Methods

Raw gene expression data, provided by Episkin, was processed with frozen RMA method [1]. Five functional groups were considered in the differential gene expression analysis: NHE, NHEK, RhE (Type 1), RhE (Type 2) and RhE (Type 3). In all following parts of analysis NHE group was used as a reference and other groups were compared to it. For the gene-level analysis, we performed Studentâ€™s T-test followed by Benjamini-Hochberg FDR adjustment [2]. For the pathway level analysis each preprocessed gene expression group was independently compared to NHE reference using an algorithm called OncoFinder [3]. Using preprocessed gene expression data as an input allows for cross-platform dataset comparison with low error rate and provides the ability to obtain functional features of intracellular regulation using mathematical estimations. Positive and negative PAS values indicate pathway up- and down-regulation, respectively. In total, we performed the analysis of 1856 signaling and 319 metabolic pathways.

Notable genes

The analysis of gene expression of the components that are involved in formation and remodeling of ECM shows that RhE samples contain highly expressed enzymes that are connected with the degradation of ECM (such as MMPs, HYALs) and low expressed enzymes that are involved in formation of ECM (HAS1, COL1, FN1). Some of the major GF associated genes are significantly upregulated in RhE (for example, GH1) compared to NHE and NHEK, while some are downregulated (FGF1, EGF). Several GF genes and genes of GF receptors may serve as a marker for NHEK (FGF1, EGF, WNT1).

Pathway Analysis

Signaling Pathways

NHE samples were selected as a norm for differential pathway analysis.
Among all examined samples NHEK samples show more perturbed pathways (1826 dysregulated pathways compared to 1784, 1765, 1768 for RhE type 1, type 2, type 3, respectively) and so these samples are more distant from NHE. Strong upregulation of Aurora B signaling pathway [4], PLK1-pathway [5], GSK3B ILK regulated pathway [6], repression of PTEN pathway [7] support the evidence that NHEK proliferate more actively than RhE samples. Also, pathway signature shows that RhE resemble mature skin with downregulation of early stage of skin development and repair processes pathways such as WNT pathway [8], EGF and EGFR pathways [9]. Growth hormone is well known to be actively involved in skin development and repair [10] and is also inhibited along with IGF. The group of Extracellular remodeling pathways is activated in NHEK, while in RhE samples it is repressed.

Metabolic Pathways

NHE demonstrated more dysregulated metabolic pathways (281 compared to 256, 246, 252 for RhE type 1, type 2, type 3 respectively).
Metabolic pathways screening showed that all studied samples have a suppressed biosynthesis of ECM skin-associated polysaccharides, such as dermatan sulfate, heparan sulfate [11]. However, RhE samples demonstrate an activation of all metabolic pathways of cholesterol biosynthesis and fatty acids metabolism. For example, we observed the activation of ester lipids and arachidonic acid (polyunsaturated omega-6 fatty acid), linoleic acid (polyunsaturated omega-6 fatty acid) metabolism signaling pathways in RhE samples. Since cholesterol, fatty acids and their esters are major components of stratum corneum [12] and all of the above mentioned pathways are not extremely perturbed compared to the reference NHE, RhE resemble the signature of a "horny layer" of skin on a pathway level. To sum up, the models of RhE resemble mature epidermis with "horny layer" on a pathway and gene expression level. RhE type 2 samples are more similar to NHE on signaling and metabolic pathway levels.

Gene expression data suffers from the "curse of dimensionality" and so requires special approaches to work with. One possible way to deal with dimensionality is to select a subset of features (genes in our case) that is related to the process. We selected 192 genes that are associated with ECM formation and remodeling and 89 core genes that are GFs or involved in regulation of GFs that are linked to the processes of skin development and proliferation.

Figure 2. ECM associated genes.

  • RhE type 1 shows similarity to native human skin on ECM associated gene expression level.
  • NHDF samples do not correlate with NHE samples and all reconstructed epidermis samples (RhE type 1, type 2, type 3 and Epiderm samples except Epiderm GSE48459 samples)
  • NHS samples and whole skin substitutes (such as RHS from GSE3204 and GSE17539 datasets) group with NHDF samples and Reticual Dermis samples on ECM associated gene expression level

Gene subsetting analysis demonstrated that samples could be divided into 2 groups that show "dermis" signature and "epidermis" signature.
Also, analysis allows to determine outliers from public domain, such as GSE48459 (EpiDerm samples) that strangely resemble "dermis" signature, while EpiDerm is a model of skin epidermis and is supposed to show "epidermis" signature.
