Methods

Raw gene expression data, provided by Episkin, was processed with frozen RMA method [McCall, Matthew N., Benjamin M. Bolstad, and Rafael A. Irizarry. "Frozen robust multiarray analysis (fRMA)." Biostatistics 11.2 (2010): 242-253.]. Differential gene Five functional groups were considered in the analysis: NHE, NHEK, RhE (Type 1), RhE (Type 2) and RhE (Type 3). In all following parts of analysis NHE group was used as a reference and other groups were compared to it. For the gene-level analysis, we performed Student’s T-test followed by Benjamini-Hochberg FDR adjustment [Benjamini, Y., and Y. Hochberg. 1995. Controlling the false discovery rate: a practical and powerful approach to multiple testing. Journal of the Royal Statistical Society B 57: 289-300.]. For pathway level analysis each preprocessed gene expression group was independently compared to NHE reference using an algorithm called OncoFinder [Buzdin AA, Zhavoronkov AA, Korzinkin MB, Venkova LS, Zenin AA, Smirnov PY, Borisov NM. Oncofinder, a new method for the analysis of intracellular signaling pathway activation using transcriptomic data. Front Genet. 2014; 5:55.]. Taking the preprocessed gene expression data as an input it allows for cross-platform dataset comparison with low error rate and has the ability to obtain functional features of intracellular regulation using mathematical estimations. Positive and negative PAS values indicate pathway up- and down-regulation, respectively. Gene expression datasets from public domain that came from different microarray platforms were preprocessed and harmonized.

  • native human skin
    • whole skin
    • epidermis
    • dermis
  • cultured skin substitutes
    • reconstructed epidermis
    • reconstructed human skin
    • epidermal skin-derived keratinocytes
    • dermal skin-derived fibroblasts

Data were profiled on different microarray platforms, so we performed harmonization in order to correct the difference between platforms.

  • NHEK_LÓreal samples correlate better with reconstructed epidermis than with native skin.

Gene expression data suffers from the "curse of dimensionality" and so requires special approaches to work with. One possible way to deal with dimensionality is to select a subset of features (genes in our case) that is related to the process. We selected 192 genes that are associated with ECM formation and remodeling and 89 core genes that are GFs or involved in regulation of GFs that are linked to the processes of skin development and proliferation.

Figure N+1 ECM associated genes.(EMC_2.PNG)

  • RhE type 1 show more similarity to native human skin on ECM associated gene expression level.
  • NHDF samples do not correlate with NHE samples and all reconstructed epidermis samples (RhE type 1, type 2, type 3 and Epiderm samples except Epiderm GSE48459 samples)
  • NHS samples and whole skin substitutes (such as RHS from GSE3204 and GSE17539 datasets) group with NHDF samples and Reticual Dermis samples on ECM associated gene expression level

Figure N+2 GFs genes

  • RhE type 2 show stronger correlation (r=0,97) with NHE LÓreal samples than RhE type 1 and type 3
Gene subsetting analysis demonstrates that samples could be divided into 2 groups that show "dermis" signature and "epidermis" signature.
Also, analysis allows to determine outliers (bad samples) from public domain, such as GSE48459 (EpiDerm samples) that resemble "dermis" signature, while EpiDerm is a model of skin epidermis and is supposed to show “epidermis” signature.
